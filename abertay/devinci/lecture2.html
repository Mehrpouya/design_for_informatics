### [Questions](https://gamedevelopment.tutsplus.com/articles/how-to-learn-board-game-design-and-development--gamedev-11607) to ask yourself - Part 1 - How many players will there be? - How long should the game be? - What choices will the player make, and when will they make them? - How will the player make these choices? - How will one player's choice impact the other players? - How will the players interact with each other?
### [Questions](https://gamedevelopment.tutsplus.com/articles/how-to-learn-board-game-design-and-development--gamedev-11607) to ask yourself - part 2 - Are there any choices that can be made by one player, but not by the others? - How does the game progress? Is it strictly turn-based, or is it in rounds with phases? - What actions will the player be able to take? - How will the outcome of an action be determined? - What is the player's goal? - How can the player win?
### More questions to ask - What emotions are you hoping to generate amongst the players? - How long are you hoping your game to take? - What discussions are you hoping to encourage? How the mechanics and narrative can help with this? - How your choice of material can create certain atmospheres? - What are the intended and unintended consequences of your design? - What is your message?
From [Games journal](http://www.thegamesjournal.com/articles/WhatIsaGame.shtml) - What should the board look like? - How many spaces should it have? - What shape do the spaces have: square, hexagonal, or round? - How are the spaces laid out? - Are all the spaces the same size or are they of different sizes? - How many pieces are there? - What do the pieces look like? Does a piece take more than one space when played?

##Think like a designer
So far you have learned various ways to critique and anlyse games, in this section we will discuss a few more techniques and things to look out for to help you to critique and analyse each others work.
###Think like a designer n.1 - Think about default scenarios that they designed? - What are the important mechanics? - What technical glitches the game has and how designers tried to resolve them! In a boardgame these can be seen as gaps in mechanics, narrative or the relationship between the two. cross player interaction,... - What were their point of views? - What is the relationship between rules and the play they create?
###Think like a designer n.2 - Try to think why the designer had to put each of those rules in their game? - How does each piece integrates into the whole story and game narrative? - How would the game change if you remove any of the rules? - Change some of the rules, play again and find out how they affect the game experience? - Identify, define and analyse metrics. These are the type of information that you collect from the user, how the designer is using them and how they are being presented in the game.
##Playtest
###When you play-test your game or another game: - try to see if the game is playable without any help from the designer. - They should be self-explanatory. - Remember you won’t be there to help people playing your board game!
###When you’re play testing: - Try to only offer the player the rule book of your game - Write down questions they ask and how they are playing your game? - Whether what they are playing is anything like what you have designed? - Try to give them as little information as possible as you are biased. - Think about why they are asking certain questions
There is always something to learn from testing your game, you need to find the right questions to ask!
The sooner you start play testing, the easier it will be to make changes. Bear in mind not to make too many dramatical changes which can result in you loosing your path and direction.
###You can’t just test your game yourself try step 1: - **friends and family:** this is still biased as they may try to avoid giving you any critical feedback ro question your dicisions, specially if you may get upset or annoyed by their feedback.
###You can’t just test your game yourself try step 2: - **Hardcore Gamers:** this again will give you a specific type of feedback. They are already familiar with many different game mechanics and has a a lot of experience of playing various video games. Their preferred genre of games may also affect their feedback as they are more specialised users.
###You can’t just test your game yourself try step 3: - **casual players** they are the ones that may help you to improve some obvious flaws in your game design. Some of you experienced this when you were reading the game manuals of the board games you’ve played.
